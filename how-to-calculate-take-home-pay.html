How to Calculate Your True Take-Home Pay From Any Job Offer

📅 May 9, 2026 ⏱ 6 min read ✍️ OfferVault Team

The number on your offer letter is not what lands in your bank account. Most people make one of the biggest financial decisions of their year — accepting a job offer — based on the gross salary number alone. This guide shows you exactly what to subtract so you know the real number before you sign anything.

⚠️ Important First Step

If you're evaluating a $90,000 offer, your actual take-home pay after federal taxes, state taxes, Social Security, Medicare, and health insurance premiums could be as low as $58,000–$64,000. That's not a small difference — that's $26,000 you need to account for.

The 6 Things That Reduce Your Salary

1

Federal Income Tax

The US uses a progressive tax system — different portions of your income are taxed at different rates. For 2026, brackets range from 10% on the first $11,600 to 37% on income above $609,350. Most professionals earning $60K–$150K pay an effective federal rate of 18%–24%.

2

State Income Tax

This varies wildly. Texas, Florida, Nevada, and Washington have zero state income tax. California can take up to 13.3%. New York tops out at 10.9%. This alone can mean a $6,000–$15,000 difference annually on the same salary depending on where you live.

3

FICA — Social Security & Medicare

These are flat deductions regardless of your state. Social Security takes 6.2% of your income up to $168,600. Medicare takes an additional 1.45%. Combined that's 7.65% off the top of every paycheck — about $4,590 per year on a $60,000 salary.

4

Health Insurance Premiums

Even with employer-sponsored coverage, the average employee pays $1,500–$6,000 per year out of pocket in premiums. Family coverage can cost $7,000–$18,000 annually in employee contributions. This money leaves your paycheck before you ever see it.

5

401(k) Contributions

If you're contributing to your retirement — which you should be — that reduces your take-home pay. The average contribution is 5%–10% of salary. On $80,000, a 6% contribution means $4,800 less per year in your pocket, though it builds your long-term wealth significantly.

6

Commute, Parking & Work Costs

These aren't deductions but they are real costs of the job. A 45-minute daily commute can cost $3,000–$8,000 per year in gas, tolls, transit passes, and parking. Work clothing, lunches, and equipment can add another $1,000–$3,000 annually.

Real Example: $90,000 Offer in Austin, TX

Let's walk through what a $90,000 offer actually becomes in your bank account:

That $90,000 offer becomes roughly $56,715 per year — about $4,726 per month in your actual bank account. This is exactly why comparing offers by gross salary alone is so misleading.

✅ Pro Tip

Always compare the take-home pay of two offers — not the gross salaries. A $80,000 offer in Texas with full health coverage can put more money in your pocket than a $95,000 offer in California with high insurance premiums and state tax.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much of my salary do I actually take home?

For most US workers earning $60,000–$120,000, you take home roughly 60%–70% of your gross salary after federal tax, state tax, FICA, and health insurance. A $90,000 salary typically results in $56,000–$65,000 in actual take-home pay depending on your state and benefits situation.

Which states have the highest take-home pay?

States with no income tax — Texas, Florida, Nevada, Washington, and Wyoming — give you the highest take-home pay on the same gross salary. California, New York, and New Jersey have the highest state income taxes, significantly reducing what you actually receive.

Does 401(k) contribution reduce my take-home pay?

Yes, traditional 401(k) contributions reduce your take-home pay but also reduce your taxable income. A 6% contribution on an $80,000 salary reduces take-home by about $4,800 per year — but also reduces your federal tax bill, so the actual net impact is slightly lower.

How do I compare two job offers with different salaries?

Calculate the real take-home pay for each offer after taxes, then subtract annual costs like health insurance premiums, commute expenses, and cost of living differences. The offer with the highest net value after all costs is the better financial choice — even if the gross salary is lower.
